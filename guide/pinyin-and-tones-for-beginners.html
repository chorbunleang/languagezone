Pinyin & the Four Tones for Beginners

Pinyin writes Chinese sounds with the Latin alphabet, and tones give each syllable its meaning. Here's a clear beginner's introduction to both.

Before you write a single character, it helps to understand how Chinese is pronounced — and that starts with pinyin and tones. Pinyin is the system that writes Mandarin sounds using the Latin alphabet, and tones are the pitch patterns that give each syllable its meaning.

What is pinyin?

Pinyin (拼音, pīnyīn) is the official romanization of Mandarin Chinese. It spells out how a character sounds using familiar letters, plus tone marks. For example, the character 你 is written nǐ in pinyin, and 好 is written hǎo — together, 你好 (nǐ hǎo) means "hello."

Every Mandarin syllable is built from an optional initial (a beginning consonant) and a final (the vowel part), carried on one of the tones. Learning pinyin first gives you a reliable way to read pronunciation while you build up your character knowledge.

The four tones (plus one)

Mandarin is a tonal language: the same syllable said with a different pitch is a different word. There are four main tones and one neutral tone, usually shown with a mark over the main vowel.

  • First tone (ˉ) — high and flat, like holding a steady note: mā.
  • Second tone (ˊ) — rising, like asking "huh?": má.
  • Third tone (ˇ) — dipping down then up: mǎ.
  • Fourth tone (ˋ) — sharp and falling, like a firm "no!": mà.
  • Neutral tone — light and short, with no mark: ma.

Why tones matter: the "ma" example

The classic example uses the syllable "ma." Depending on the tone, it becomes an entirely different word:

  • mā — 妈 — mother
  • má — 麻 — hemp
  • mǎ — 马 — horse
  • mà — 骂 — to scold
  • ma — 吗 — a question particle

Say them with the wrong pitch and you may accidentally call your mother a horse. Tones are not optional decoration — they are part of the word.

A helpful way to feel the tones: first tone is flat like a sung note, second rises like a question, third dips like you're unsure, and fourth falls like a command. Exaggerate them at first — you can relax later.

Where does the tone mark go?

The tone mark sits over the main vowel of the syllable. A simple order of priority: mark an a or e if present; otherwise the o in "ou"; otherwise the last vowel. So we write hǎo, xué, and guó.

Tips for practising tones

  • Learn each new word with its tone, not as an afterthought — the tone is part of the word.
  • Say it out loud. Tones live in your voice, not on the page.
  • Exaggerate at first. Big, clear tones are easier to hear and correct.
  • Pair sound with writing. When you practise characters, say the pinyin and tone aloud as you write.

Bring it together with writing

Pinyin tells you how a character sounds; handwriting helps you remember its shape and meaning. The two reinforce each other.
