How to Practise Chinese Handwriting

Writing characters by hand is one of the most effective ways to learn Chinese. This guide covers everything a beginner needs: why it works, the stroke-order rules, how to use grid paper, and how to build a habit that lasts.

Why write by hand at all?

In an age of typing and pinyin input, it is fair to ask whether handwriting still matters. For learning, it clearly does. Forming each stroke by hand forces you to notice the exact shape and structure of a character — which parts sit where, how big each piece is, and in what order the strokes flow. That active attention builds memory far more strongly than passively reading.

Start with stroke order

Chinese characters are written in a fixed order of strokes, and following it makes characters faster to write, more balanced, and easier to remember. A few simple rules cover most characters:

  • Top to bottom. Write upper strokes before lower ones.
  • Left to right. Write left strokes before right ones.
  • Horizontal before vertical. In 十 (ten), the horizontal line comes first.
  • Outside before inside. Draw the outer frame before the contents.
  • Close the box last. In 国, write the enclosure, then the inside, then the bottom sealing stroke.
  • Centre before wings. In symmetrical characters like 小, the middle stroke often comes first.

Use grid paper — the 田字格

Chinese learners practise on special grid paper. The most common is the 田字格 (tián zì gé), a square divided by a faint cross so you can place each part of a character in proportion. Another style, the 米字格 (mǐ zì gé), adds diagonal lines for extra guidance. Our Worksheet Maker lets you choose between all of these.

The trace-then-write method

  • Trace first. Start each row with faded characters and trace over them to learn the shape and order.
  • Then write from memory. Fill the empty squares yourself, checking against the model character.
  • Say it as you write. Speak the pinyin and meaning aloud to link sound, meaning, and movement.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Rushing. Slow, careful strokes teach your hand the correct shape.
  • Ignoring proportion. Use the grid lines to keep the halves balanced.
  • Skipping stroke order. It feels optional, but it leads to awkward characters and slows you down.
  • Practising once and moving on. Short, repeated sessions beat one long marathon.

Build a small daily habit

Consistency matters more than volume. Ten focused minutes a day — a single worksheet of five to ten characters, traced and then written from memory — will take you a long way over a few months.
