Simplified vs Traditional Chinese

Both write the same language, but they look different and are used in different places. Here's the difference — and which one to learn.

Early on, most learners meet a fork in the road: should you learn Simplified or Traditional Chinese characters? Both write the same language, but they look different, and they're used in different places. Here's what sets them apart and how to choose.

What are they?

Traditional characters are the older forms, used for centuries. Simplified characters were introduced in mainland China in the mid-20th century to make characters easier to write and learning faster, by reducing the number of strokes in many common characters.

For example, the character for "horse" is 馬 in Traditional and 马 in Simplified; "country" is 國 versus 国; "to speak" is 說 versus 说. Many characters are actually identical in both systems.

Where is each used?

  • Simplified: mainland China and Singapore.
  • Traditional: Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Macau.

Because mainland China has by far the largest population of speakers and learners, Simplified is the more widely encountered system worldwide, and it's what most textbooks and courses for foreigners teach.

Which should you learn?

  • Learn Simplified if you're focused on mainland China, doing business there, or just want the most widely used system and the gentlest start. This suits most beginners.
  • Learn Traditional if you have ties to Taiwan or Hong Kong, are interested in classical texts and calligraphy, or plan to live in a region that uses it.
You don't have to choose forever. Many learners start with Simplified and later pick up Traditional forms as they read more — the spoken language and grammar are the same, so it's mainly a matter of recognising alternate written forms.
